
# Invariant Systems Theory
### A General Theory of System Integrity Under Distortion

Author: Nicholas D'Zilva  
Year: 2026  

---

## Overview

This repository contains the foundational work for **Invariant Systems Theory** — a general framework describing how complex systems lose integrity and ultimately fail.

The theory applies across domains:
- Artificial Intelligence
- Economic systems
- Institutions and governance
- Communication systems
- Regulatory frameworks

It proposes that system failure is not primarily a function of capacity, but of **degraded self-correction under distortion**.

---

## Core Claim

A system has integrity when:

- Its correction mechanism remains independent of its distortion field  
- Its feedback remains coupled to observable reality  
- Its accumulated divergence remains within recoverable bounds  
- Its update rule preserves causal structure under constraint variation  

A system fails when it loses the ability to detect and correct its own divergence — while continuing to operate as though it has not.

---

## The Failure Mechanism

System degradation occurs through four coupled processes:

1. **Loss of Independence**  
   The correction mechanism becomes entangled with the distortion it is meant to correct.

2. **Feedback Corruption**  
   Signals from reality are suppressed, delayed, or distorted.

3. **Distortion Accumulation**  
   Divergence from the least-distorted achievable state increases over time.

4. **Causal Degradation**  
   Real mechanisms are replaced by proxy signals optimised for measurement rather than reality.

These processes reinforce each other, producing a self-accelerating failure trajectory.

---

## Failure Modes

Three levels of system failure are defined:

- **Type I Drift**: Parameter error, causal structure intact (recoverable)  
- **Type II Drift**: Partial causal corruption (degraded generalisation)  
- **Type III Drift**: Proxy replaces mechanism (structural failure)  

Type III drift represents a critical threshold where the system no longer models reality — it models its own signals.

---

## Key Concepts

- **Baseline Reference State (B_ref)**  
  The least-distorted achievable representation under minimal interference.

- **Detection Gap (Δ)**  
  The gap between perceived system state and actual system condition.

- **Distortion Load**  
  The accumulated divergence the system must correct.

- **Irreversibility Barrier**  
  The point beyond which internal correction is no longer sufficient.

- **Integrity**  
  The capacity of a system to observe and correct its own divergence from reality.

---

## Implications

This framework suggests:

- Systems can appear stable while failing structurally  
- Performance metrics can mask underlying degradation  
- Compliance and guardrails do not guarantee integrity  
- Moral or behavioural instruction does not prevent structural drift  

The primary failure mode is not visible collapse — but **undetected divergence**.

---

## Relation to AI Systems

In machine learning systems, this manifests as:

- Reward hacking  
- Proxy optimisation  
- Hallucination under confidence  
- Alignment drift under distribution shift  

Invariant Systems Theory reframes alignment as:

> Preservation of causal structure under constraint variation

Rather than behavioural correctness.
